One of the first questions expats ask when considering a move to Antigua Guatemala is whether they actually need a car. The short answer is probably not. This compact colonial city was designed centuries before automobiles existed, and its walkable streets, affordable tuk-tuks, and reliable shuttle network make car-free living surprisingly easy.

Here is what you need to know about getting around Antigua Guatemala without a car.

## Walking Is the Best Way to Get Around Town

Antigua's historic center is roughly 1.5 kilometers across. You can walk from one end to the other in about 20 minutes. The flat grid layout makes navigation straightforward, and the pedestrian-friendly streets are one of the biggest draws for people relocating here.

Most expats find that daily errands, grocery runs, restaurant visits, and social outings all happen within walking distance. The cobblestone streets are charming but uneven, so investing in comfortable walking shoes is worth it. Many residents keep a light rain jacket in their bag during the rainy season (May through October) since afternoon showers can pop up quickly.

Walking also lets you discover the small details that make Antigua special, hidden courtyards, street vendors selling fresh fruit, and the way Volcán de Agua shifts color as the light changes through the day.

## Tuk-Tuks: Antigua's Signature Ride

The three-walled motorized rickshaws known as tuk-tuks are everywhere in Antigua, and they are the go-to transportation for trips that are too far to walk or when you are carrying groceries. You can flag one down on almost any street, or call ahead for a pickup.

**Typical fares within town:** Q15 to Q25 (about $2 to $3 USD) for most destinations in the city center. Trips to the outskirts or nearby villages may run Q30 to Q50.

A few tips for using tuk-tuks:

- **Negotiate the fare before you get in.** There are no meters, so agree on a price upfront. Most drivers are honest, but it avoids misunderstandings.
- **Carry small bills.** Drivers often do not have change for Q100 notes. Keep Q20s and Q10s handy.
- **Download a contact number.** Many expats develop a relationship with a favorite tuk-tuk driver and save their WhatsApp number for regular pickups.
- **They are not great for heavy cargo.** If you are doing a big grocery haul at La Bodegona or Walmart, consider a taxi or delivery service instead.

## Chicken Buses and Local Buses

Guatemala's famous "chicken buses" (repurposed American school buses) run fixed routes between Antigua and surrounding towns. They are incredibly cheap, usually Q5 to Q10 per ride, and a genuine cultural experience. The buses are colorful, loud, and crowded during peak hours.

For daily commutes to nearby towns like Ciudad Vieja, San Miguel Dueñas, or Jocotenango, local buses work well. They run frequently during the morning and evening rush, with reduced service midday and on weekends.

**A few things to know:**

- Pickpocketing can happen on crowded buses. Keep your phone and wallet in front pockets or a cross-body bag.
- Buses stop anywhere along the route. Just flag one down to board, and tell the ayudante (assistant) when you want to get off.
- They are not air-conditioned, and they can get packed. Dress light and bring water.

For longer distances, first-class "pullman" buses are a much more comfortable option. Companies like Litegua and ADN connect Antigua to Guatemala City, the Pacific coast, and other destinations with reserved seating and luggage compartments.

## Shared Shuttles for Day Trips and Travel

Tourist shuttles are the sweet spot between chicken buses and private transfers. Several companies in Antigua run shared minivan shuttles to popular destinations:

- **Lake Atitlán:** About 2.5 hours, $12 to $25 USD
- **Guatemala City airport:** About 1 hour, $10 to $20 USD
- **Xela (Quetzaltenango):** About 3 hours, $15 to $25 USD
- **Semuc Champey:** About 8 hours, $25 to $35 USD

Book through your hotel, a local travel agency, or online platforms like GuateGo. Shuttles usually pick up from your accommodation and drop you at your destination door. They are reliable, safe, and far more convenient than navigating chicken bus transfers with luggage.

## Taxis and Ride-Hailing

Regular taxis operate in Antigua, though they are less common than tuk-tuks for short trips. For longer rides, especially to or from Guatemala City, a private taxi is comfortable and reasonably priced (around Q200 to Q350 to the airport, depending on negotiation).

Uber operates in Guatemala City and can sometimes be called from Antigua, though availability is inconsistent. Some expats use the InDriver app, which lets you propose a fare and negotiate with drivers.

For airport transfers, many people prefer pre-arranged private drivers. Your property manager, hotel, or real estate agent can usually recommend a trusted driver.

## Biking Around Antigua

Cycling is growing in popularity, especially among expats who live slightly outside the center. The terrain is mostly flat within town, and the mild climate makes year-round biking comfortable. Mountain biking is also popular in the surrounding hills and coffee farms.

A few things to keep in mind:

- Cobblestone streets are rough on road bike tires. Mountain bikes or fat-tire bikes handle them better.
- Bike theft is a real concern. Invest in a solid U-lock and never leave your bike unattended for long.
- Traffic can be chaotic, especially around the bus terminal and market areas. Ride defensively.
- Several shops in town sell and rent bikes, including electric models that make the hilly outskirts easier to navigate.

## Do You Ever Actually Need a Car?

There are a few situations where having a car makes sense:

- **Regular trips to Guatemala City** for business, embassy visits, or shopping at big-box stores.
- **Living outside of town** in areas like El Hato, San Lucas Sacatepéquez, or along the road to Pastores, where public transport is limited.
- **Weekend exploration** of remote areas like the Pacific coast, Semuc Champey, or the highland markets.

If you only need a car occasionally, renting is straightforward. Several rental agencies operate in Antigua and at the Guatemala City airport. Daily rates start around $35 to $50 USD for a basic sedan. International companies like Hertz and Budget have airport locations, while local agencies in Antigua often offer better rates for weekly or monthly rentals.

## The Bottom Line

Antigua Guatemala is one of the few places in Central America where you can genuinely live well without a car. The combination of walkability, affordable tuk-tuks, and a solid shuttle network covers most daily needs and travel plans. Many long-term expats go years without owning a vehicle and never feel limited.
