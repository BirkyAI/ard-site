Living in Antigua Guatemala means having some of Central America's most stunning destinations just a short drive away. Whether you are a new expat still settling in or a long-term resident looking for a weekend escape, these day trips offer incredible variety without requiring an overnight stay.

## Pacaya Volcano: Lava Fields and Sunset Views

At just 45 minutes from central Antigua, Pacaya is the most accessible volcano for a day trip. The hike takes about two hours each way and is moderate enough for most fitness levels. You will walk through pine forests before reaching the barren lava fields at the top, where steam vents remind you that this volcano is very much alive.

The best time to go is late afternoon so you can catch the sunset from the summit. Local guides at the trailhead charge around Q100 ($13 USD) per person. Bring a jacket because it gets cold and windy at the top, even on warm days.

Pro tip: You can roast marshmallows over the volcanic vents near the summit. Several tour operators in Antigua sell Pacaya packages that include transportation, guide, and snacks for about $35 to $50 per person.

## Coffee Farm Tours in the Highlands

Guatemala produces some of the world's best coffee, and several working farms around Antigua welcome visitors for tours. Finca Filadelfia, located about 15 minutes from town, offers a full experience that includes walking through the coffee plants, learning the processing methods, and tasting freshly roasted beans.

For something more intimate, smaller farms in the villages of San Miguel Dueñas and Ciudad Vieja offer personal tours where the farmer walks you through the entire bean-to-cup process. These tours typically cost $15 to $25 and last about two hours.

The coffee harvest runs from November through March, which is the most interesting time to visit because you can see the picking and processing in action.

## Lake Atitlan: Guatemala's Crown Jewel

Yes, Lake Atitlan is technically a full-day trip from Antigua (about 2.5 to 3 hours each way), but many expats make the drive regularly because the lake is simply that beautiful. The three towering volcanoes surrounding the lake create a landscape that Aldous Huxley once called "the most beautiful lake in the world."

The most common day-trip route is to drive to Panajachel, the main lake town, and take a boat across to San Pedro La Laguna or San Marcos. San Pedro has a lively expat scene with great restaurants and a relaxed vibe. San Marcos is quieter, known for yoga retreats and natural medicine shops.

Leave Antigua by 7 AM to maximize your lake time. You can be back by 7 or 8 PM if you avoid driving after dark (which is strongly recommended on Guatemalan highways).

## Chichicastenango Market

Every Thursday and Sunday, the highland town of Chichicastenango hosts one of the largest and most colorful indigenous markets in all of Central America. Vendors sell everything from handwoven textiles and carved wooden masks to fresh produce and medicinal herbs.

The market is about two hours from Antigua, making it a comfortable day trip. Arrive early (by 9 AM) to see the market at its liveliest. The famous Santo Tomas Church sits right in the middle of the market, where Mayan spiritual leaders still perform ceremonies on the church steps.

Budget about Q50 to Q100 ($7 to $13) for the chicken bus experience, or hire a private shuttle for around $25 to $35 per person each way. Many expats prefer the shuttle for comfort and reliability.

## Hobbitenango: A Mountain Escape Close to Home

Just 20 minutes above Antigua on the slopes of Volcan de Agua, Hobbitenango is an eco-park with panoramic views, quirky hobbit-hole architecture, and hiking trails. It is the easiest day trip on this list because you barely leave town.

The park has a restaurant, playground areas for kids, and several walking paths with stunning views of Antigua and the surrounding volcanoes. Entry costs about Q50 ($7 USD). It is a favorite spot for expat families on weekends.

## Monterrico Beach and the Pacific Coast

If you crave ocean air, Monterrico is the closest beach to Antigua at about 1.5 to 2 hours by car. The black volcanic sand is distinctive, and the Pacific surf is warm year-round. The drive takes you through flat coastal farmland, a completely different landscape from the highlands.

Several beachfront restaurants serve fresh seafood, and you can rent a palapa with lounge chairs for the day. During turtle nesting season (June through November), conservation groups organize nighttime releases where baby turtles are returned to the sea.

The road to Monterrico includes a river crossing by ferry (lancha), which adds a fun adventure element to the trip. The ferry costs a few dollars per vehicle and runs throughout the day.

## Guatemala City: Museums, Malls, and Urban Culture

Many Antigua expats overlook Guatemala City, but the capital has world-class museums, excellent international restaurants, and shopping that Antigua cannot match. The Museo Popol Vuh houses one of the finest collections of Maya art in the world. The Ixchel Museum of Indigenous Textiles is another gem.

Zona 10 (the Zona Viva) is the safest and most visitor-friendly area, with upscale restaurants, coffee shops, and the Oakland Mall. The drive from Antigua takes about one hour on the highway, or you can hire a private driver for the day for around $40 to $60.

## Practical Tips for Day Trips from Antigua

**Transportation:** Renting a car gives you the most freedom, but many expats rely on private shuttles and tour operators. Uber works in Guatemala City but is limited in rural areas.

**Safety:** Stick to main roads, avoid driving after dark, and keep valuables out of sight in your vehicle. These same rules apply whether you are driving yourself or taking a shuttle.

**Weather:** The dry season (November through April) is the best time for volcano hikes and market visits. The rainy season (May through October) typically brings afternoon showers, so plan outdoor activities for the morning.

**What to bring:** Sunscreen, a rain jacket, cash in small denominations, and comfortable walking shoes will cover most day trips. For volcano hikes, add layers and a headlamp.

Living in Antigua Guatemala puts you at the center of an incredibly diverse landscape. Within a two-hour radius you have volcanoes, lakes, beaches, indigenous markets, coffee farms, and a modern capital city. That variety is one of the biggest reasons expats who move here end up staying.
