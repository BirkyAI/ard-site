Driving in Guatemala as a foreigner is completely legal with your home country license, and most expats in Antigua Guatemala get around just fine without ever switching to a local one. That said, the roads, driving culture, and traffic rules here are different from what you might be used to, so a little preparation goes a long way.

## Can You Drive with a Foreign License in Guatemala?

Yes. Guatemala recognizes valid foreign driver's licenses for visitors and residents alike. You do not need an International Driving Permit, though carrying one never hurts. Your home country license, along with your passport, is sufficient if you get pulled over at a checkpoint.

If you plan to stay long-term and want a Guatemalan license, the process is straightforward but bureaucratic. You will need your passport, a valid foreign license, proof of residence (your utility bill or rental contract works), and a medical exam from a local clinic. The exam costs around Q100 to Q200 and takes about 30 minutes. Head to the municipal traffic office in Guatemala City or the nearest SAT office. The whole process takes a morning, and the license is valid for one to two years.

## What Are the Roads Like Around Antigua Guatemala?

The roads in and around Antigua Guatemala vary wildly. The Pan-American Highway (CA-1) connecting Antigua to Guatemala City is a well-maintained, divided highway. The road to Ciudad Vieja and San Miguel Dueñas is paved and generally in good shape. But head into the hills toward El Hato or San Mateo Milpas Altas, and you will encounter narrow, winding dirt roads with steep grades.

During rainy season (May through October), unpaved roads can get slippery and develop deep ruts. A vehicle with decent ground clearance is recommended if you plan to live outside the town center. Many expats opt for a small SUV or pickup truck, though plenty of people manage with a sedan if they stick to the main roads.

Speed bumps (called "topes" or "reductores") are everywhere, and they are often unmarked. Hitting one at full speed can damage your suspension, so keep your eyes on the road, especially entering small towns.

## Traffic Rules and Driving Culture

Guatemalan traffic laws exist on paper, but enforcement and adherence are inconsistent. Here is what to expect:

- **Stop signs and traffic lights** are treated more as suggestions in rural areas. At intersections without lights, the general rule is the bigger vehicle goes first.
- **Passing on two-lane roads** is common, even on curves and hills. Be defensive and expect oncoming traffic to drift into your lane.
- **Turn signals** are rarely used. Watch the vehicle's position and speed to gauge what other drivers are doing.
- **Night driving** is risky outside of cities. Many vehicles lack proper headlights, pedestrians wear dark clothing, and livestock sometimes wanders onto the road.
- **Police checkpoints** are routine. Carry your license, passport copy, and vehicle registration. Officers are generally polite and wave you through.

## Buying or Renting a Car in Guatemala

Renting is the easiest option for your first few months. Daily rates start around Q250 to Q400 for a basic sedan. Local agencies in Antigua often offer better rates than international chains. Monthly rentals can drop to Q4,000 to Q6,000.

Buying a car in Guatemala is a viable long-term option. Popular models include the Toyota Hilux, Suzuki Swift, and Hyundai Tucson. Prices for used vehicles are comparable to or slightly higher than US prices due to import taxes. The transfer process requires a notary, and you should always verify the vehicle has no outstanding liens through the Registro de la Propiedad.

## Gasoline and Fuel Costs

Gas stations (called "gasolineras") are plentiful along main highways but scarce in rural highlands. Guatemala uses regular and super unleaded gasoline, and prices hover around Q30 to Q35 per liter (roughly $4 per gallon). Most stations are full-service, and attendants pump the gas for you. Tipping Q5 is customary but not required.

## Tips for Driving in Antigua Guatemala as an Expat

- Always carry copies of your documents, not originals
- Avoid driving at night outside of town until you know the roads
- Invest in a dashcam for insurance and dispute purposes
- Learn basic Spanish phrases for checkpoints ("Buenos días, oficiale. Aquí está mi licencia.")
- Budget for vehicle maintenance, as rough roads wear parts faster
- Join local expat Facebook groups for real-time road condition updates

Driving in Antigua Guatemala gives you the freedom to explore at your own pace, from weekend trips to Lake Atitlán to daily runs to the market in Chichi. With the right preparation and a defensive driving mindset, most expats find it becomes second nature within a few weeks.
