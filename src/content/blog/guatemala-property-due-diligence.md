Buying property in a foreign country is exciting, but skipping due diligence can turn your dream into a legal nightmare. In Guatemala, the process works differently than in the US or Canada, and what you don't know can absolutely hurt you.

This checklist walks you through every step of property due diligence in Antigua Guatemala, from the initial title search to the final notary signature. Whether you are eyeing a colonial fixer-upper or a pre-construction villa, these are the boxes you need to check.

## Start With the Title Search

The single most important step in any Guatemalan property purchase is verifying clean title. Guatemala uses a centralized property registry system called the Registro General de la Propiedad (RGP). Every legitimate property transaction must be registered here.

Your lawyer should pull a full extract from the RGP for the property you want to buy. This document shows the current owner, any liens, mortgages, easements, or legal disputes attached to the property. It also shows the property boundaries and lot number.

**Red flags to watch for:**

- Liens or encumbrances that the seller hasn't mentioned
- Multiple owners listed (common with inherited properties)
- Boundary descriptions that don't match what you see on the ground
- Recent ownership changes that suggest a quick flip or dispute

In Antigua Guatemala specifically, some colonial properties have complex inheritance histories. Families pass homes down through generations without always updating the registry. A property might list three or four siblings as co-owners, and you need every single one to sign off on the sale.

## Verify Municipal Records

Beyond the national property registry, you need to check with the local municipality (Municipalidad de Antigua Guatemala) for:

**Zoning and land use permits.** Antigua has strict building codes because of its UNESCO World Heritage status. The municipality controls what you can and cannot do with a property. A beautiful ruin might seem perfect for restoration, but if the zoning doesn't allow commercial use, your boutique hotel dreams are dead before they start.

**Property tax status.** Request a certificate showing the property is current on IUSI (Impuesto Único Sobre Inmuebles) payments. Outstanding property taxes become the new owner's problem in Guatemala. This is one of the most commonly missed steps.

**Construction permits.** If the property has any recent additions or modifications, verify that proper permits were filed. Unpermitted construction can lead to fines or even demolition orders. In Antigua's historic center, the Consejo Nacional para la Protección de la Antigua Guatemala (CNPAG) has additional oversight for historic structures.

## Hire the Right Lawyer

This cannot be overstated: you need a Guatemalan attorney who specializes in real estate transactions and has experience working with foreign buyers. Do not use the seller's lawyer. Do not use a general practice attorney who "also does real estate."

A good real estate lawyer in Antigua will:

- Conduct the full title search at the RGP
- Verify all municipal records and tax certificates
- Draft or review the purchase agreement (promesa de compraventa)
- Coordinate with the notary for the final deed transfer
- Ensure compliance with foreign ownership rules

**What to budget:** Legal fees for a standard property purchase in Antigua typically run $1,500 to $3,000 USD. This is not the place to save money. The cost of a legal dispute far exceeds the cost of proper representation.

## Understand the Notary System

Guatemala uses a notary system that is very different from what North Americans are used to. In Guatemala, a notary public is a licensed attorney with special authorization to authenticate legal documents. The notary does not just stamp papers. They are responsible for verifying the legality of the transaction and registering it with the RGP.

The final deed (escritura pública) must be signed before a licensed notary. Both the buyer and seller (or their legal representatives with power of attorney) must be present. The notary then registers the deed with the property registry.

**Important:** The notary's role in Guatemala carries legal weight. If something goes wrong with the transaction, the notary shares liability. This is actually a protection for you as a buyer, because it means the notary has a professional obligation to verify everything is in order.

## Check for Environmental and Geological Risks

Antigua Guatemala sits in a valley surrounded by three volcanoes and sits in a seismically active zone. Due diligence should include:

**Volcanic risk assessment.** Properties closer to Volcán de Fuego (the active one) may face periodic ashfall. While most properties in Antigua proper are at a safe distance, properties in areas like El Hato or along the southern slopes may require additional consideration.

**Seismic compliance.** Guatemala has building codes for earthquake resistance, but enforcement varies. Ask your lawyer to verify that the construction meets current seismic standards, especially for older colonial structures.

**Flooding and drainage.** During rainy season (May through October), some low-lying areas in and around Antigua experience drainage issues. Visit the property during or just after heavy rain if possible.

## Get a Professional Property Inspection

Professional home inspections are not yet standard practice in Guatemala, but they are absolutely worth the investment. Hire an independent engineer or architect to evaluate:

- Structural integrity, especially for colonial-era buildings
- Roof condition (flat roofs in Antigua can leak during rainy season)
- Electrical systems (older properties may have outdated wiring)
- Plumbing and water supply (municipal water vs. well vs. spring)
- Foundation condition (volcanic soil can shift over time)

An inspection typically costs $300 to $500 USD and can save you tens of thousands in hidden repairs.

## Understand Foreign Ownership Rules

Good news: foreigners can own property in Guatemala with very few restrictions. You do not need residency or citizenship. You can buy property on a tourist visa.

However, there are a few rules to know:

- Properties within a certain distance of borders and coastlines have restrictions (this does not affect Antigua)
- You need a Guatemalan tax identification number (NIT) for the transaction
- All documents must be in Spanish or officially translated

Your lawyer can help you obtain the NIT and handle the translation requirements.

## The Purchase Agreement Timeline

A typical property purchase in Antigua follows this timeline:

1. **Offer and negotiation** (1 to 2 weeks)
2. **Promesa de compraventa signed** (purchase promise with deposit, usually 10%)
3. **Due diligence period** (2 to 4 weeks for title search, inspections, municipal checks)
4. **Final deed signing before notary** (both parties present)
5. **Registration with RGP** (2 to 6 weeks for processing)

The entire process from offer to registered deed takes 6 to 12 weeks on average. Pre-construction purchases follow a different timeline with phased payments.

## Your Due Diligence Checklist

Here is the condensed version you can take to your lawyer:

- [ ] Full title search at RGP (clean title, no liens)
- [ ] Municipal property tax certificate (IUSI current)
- [ ] Zoning verification with municipality
- [ ] Construction permits for any modifications
- [ ] CNPAG approval for historic center properties
- [ ] Professional property inspection
- [ ] Environmental risk check (volcano, seismic, flooding)
- [ ] NIT obtained for foreign buyer
- [ ] Independent legal representation hired
- [ ] Purchase agreement reviewed by your lawyer

Due diligence takes time and costs money, but it is the difference between a smart investment and an expensive mistake. Antigua Guatemala offers incredible real estate opportunities for foreign buyers, and with the right preparation, you can buy with confidence.
