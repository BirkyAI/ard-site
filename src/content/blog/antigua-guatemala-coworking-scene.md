Antigua Guatemala has quietly become one of Central America's most attractive destinations for remote workers and digital nomads. With reliable high-speed internet, a growing number of coworking spaces, a low cost of living, and a backdrop of colonial architecture framed by three volcanoes, it is no surprise that the coworking scene here has exploded in recent years.

Whether you are a freelancer, startup founder, or remote employee looking for a change of pace, Antigua offers a compelling blend of productivity and lifestyle that few cities can match.

## Why Digital Nomads Choose Antigua Guatemala

The appeal starts with practicality. Guatemala's cost of living is significantly lower than most North American and European cities — a remote worker earning a typical Western salary can live very comfortably here. A furnished apartment in a safe neighborhood runs $600 to $1,200 per month, meals at excellent local restaurants cost $5 to $15, and a high-speed fiber internet plan is around $30 to $50 monthly.

Beyond the numbers, Antigua delivers something harder to quantify: quality of life. The city is walkable, the climate is springlike year-round, and the expat community is large enough to feel welcoming without overwhelming the local culture. Many remote workers arrive planning to stay for a month and end up settling for a year or more.

The timezone is also a major advantage for anyone working with North American clients. Guatemala operates on Central Standard Time (UTC-6), which aligns perfectly with US business hours — no more late-night calls or early-morning scrambles.

## Top Coworking Spaces in Antigua

Antigua's coworking options have matured well beyond a few cafés with decent Wi-Fi. Here are the main spaces where the remote work community gathers:

### Impact Hub Antigua

Located in the heart of the historic center, Impact Hub brings its global network to Antigua with a modern, professionally managed space. Expect fast fiber internet, ergonomic seating, private meeting rooms, and a community of entrepreneurs and social impact workers. Day passes and monthly memberships are available, with monthly rates typically ranging from $100 to $180 depending on access level.

### Selina Antigua

Part of the international Selina hostel and coworking chain, this space caters specifically to digital nomads. The setup includes dedicated desks, comfortable lounge areas, and a rooftop with volcano views — arguably the most inspiring Zoom background you will ever have. Memberships include access to events, workshops, and a global network of Selina locations. Monthly coworking passes run approximately $150 to $200.

### Local Cafés with Strong Wi-Fi

Antigua's café culture is thriving, and many establishments have embraced the remote worker crowd. Spots like Café Bohème, Y Tu Piña También, and Fernando's Kaffee offer reliable internet, quality coffee, and an atmosphere conducive to focused work. This is not a substitute for a proper coworking space if you need quiet and stability, but it is a perfect complement for lighter work days.

For many remote workers, the ideal routine blends a few days at a coworking space with café sessions and work-from-home days — and Antigua's compact size makes this effortless.

## Internet Reliability: The Make-or-Break Factor

One of the most common concerns about working remotely from Central America is internet quality. In Antigua, this is largely a non-issue. The city has access to fiber optic networks from providers like Tigo and Claro, with speeds commonly reaching 100 to 300 Mbps in well-connected buildings. Coworking spaces typically invest in business-grade connections with backup solutions.

That said, not every rental property has fiber. When choosing a place to live, confirming the internet setup should be a top priority. ## The Cost of Coworking in Antigua

Coworking in Antigua is remarkably affordable compared to major cities:

- **Day pass:** $10 to $20 USD
- **Weekly pass:** $40 to $70 USD
- **Monthly membership:** $100 to $200 USD
- **Dedicated desk (monthly):** $150 to $250 USD
- **Private office:** $300 to $500 USD

These rates include amenities like high-speed internet, coffee, printing, and access to community events. For context, a comparable coworking membership in New York, London, or San Francisco would cost three to five times as much.

Many remote workers find that the savings on rent, food, and coworking alone can cover the cost of their flight to Guatemala — and that is before factoring in the lifestyle upgrade.

## Building a Community: Networking and Social Life

Working remotely can be isolating anywhere, but Antigua's digital nomad community is active and welcoming. Regular meetups, language exchange events, and informal gatherings happen throughout the week. Facebook groups and WhatsApp communities for expats in Antigua are very active — joining a few before you arrive is a great way to start connecting.

Many coworking spaces organize their own events: skill-sharing workshops, networking happy hours, and even weekend excursions to nearby attractions like Lake Atitlán or the Pacaya volcano. The community is diverse — you will meet software developers, writers, designers, consultants, and entrepreneurs from every corner of the globe.

For those staying longer, volunteering opportunities abound. Teaching English, supporting local NGOs, or contributing to community development projects are popular ways to put down roots and feel genuinely connected to the place you are calling home.

## Where to Live: Neighborhoods for Remote Workers

Choosing the right neighborhood can make or break your remote work experience in Antigua. The most popular areas for digital nomads include:

**Centro Histórico** — Walking distance to everything: coworking spaces, cafés, restaurants, and the central park. Properties here tend to be smaller and pricier, but the convenience is unmatched.

**Barrio de La Concepción** — A quieter residential neighborhood just a few blocks from the center. You get more space for your money while still being close to the action.

**San Felipe de Jesús** — Slightly further from the tourist center but increasingly popular with long-term residents. Larger properties, more greenery, and a more local feel.

**El Hato** — A hillside village above Antigua with stunning volcano views and cooler temperatures. Perfect for those who want to be immersed in nature while still being a short drive from the city. Several beautiful rental properties here offer the kind of setting that makes you wonder why you ever worked in a glass tower.

## Practical Tips for Working Remotely from Antigua

Before you pack your laptop and book a flight, here are a few things worth knowing:

**Visa situation.** Most nationalities receive a 90-day tourist visa on arrival. If you plan to stay longer, you can do a border run to Mexico or El Salvador, or apply for a longer-term visa. Guatemala does not yet have a specific digital nomad visa, but the existing options are workable for most remote workers.

**Banking.** Opening a local bank account is possible but involves paperwork. Most remote workers use international banking solutions like Wise or Revolut for daily expenses and keep their home bank account for salary deposits. Read our [guide to Guatemala banking for expats](/blog/guatemala-banking-for-expats/) for more details.

**Healthcare.** Guatemala has both public and private healthcare systems. Private clinics in Antigua and nearby Guatemala City offer quality care at a fraction of US prices. Many expats opt for international health insurance. Our [healthcare guide for expats](/blog/antigua-guatemala-healthcare-expats/) covers everything you need to know.

**Safety.** Antigua is one of the safest cities in Guatemala, with a strong police presence and a tight-knit community. Standard precautions apply — avoid flashing expensive electronics, use registered taxis at night, and stay aware of your surroundings. Most remote workers report feeling very safe here.

**Time to settle in.** Give yourself at least two to three weeks to find your rhythm. The first week is often spent setting up logistics (SIM card, coworking membership, neighborhood exploration). By week two, most people have a comfortable routine in place.
